# Email Structure Skill

## The 5-Part Email Anatomy

Every professional email has five components. Each has one job. Don't let them bleed into each other.

---

### 1. Subject Line
**Job:** Get the email opened. Nothing else.

Rules:
- Specific over clever — "UX Research Contract Opportunity" beats "Quick question"
- Set accurate expectations — don't bait-and-switch
- 6–10 words max
- No ALL CAPS, excessive punctuation, or emoji in B2B contexts

Antipatterns to avoid:
- "Touching base" → says nothing
- "Following up" → generic, feels automated
- "I have a question for you" → wastes the slot

---

### 2. Salutation
**Job:** Open with the person's name. Make it feel human.

Rules:
- Use their first name: "Hello Amal," or "Hi Marcus,"
- Never: "Dear Sir/Madam," "To Whom It May Concern," "Hey there,"
- One line, period or comma — don't overthink it

---

### 3. Body
**Job:** Deliver the full context. Two paragraphs max for most emails.

Structure:
- **Para 1 — Why you're reaching out.** Context + the specific reason this person is relevant. One clear problem or opportunity.
- **Para 2 — The specifics.** Timeline, scope, details, constraints. What they need to know to decide.

Rules:
- No filler openers: "I hope this message finds you well" → cut it. Start with substance.
- Each sentence must earn its place — if it doesn't add information, delete it.
- Simple language. Short sentences. No jargon unless the recipient would use it themselves.
- Match length to complexity — a simple ask gets one paragraph; a complex proposal gets two or three, never more without a reason.

---

### 4. Closing
**Job:** Tell them the one next step. Make it low-friction.

Rules:
- One CTA only — no options, no "or alternatively…"
- Make the action easy: "let me know if you're interested" or "reply with a time that works"
- Don't oversell in the closing — the body did that job
- Keep it one sentence

Examples:
- "Let me know if you're open to a quick call — I can work around your schedule."
- "Reply with any questions and I'll send over the details."
- "If this sounds like a fit, I can send a brief overview to review first."

---

### 5. Signature
**Job:** Establish identity and credibility. Nothing more.

Format:
```
[First + Last Name]
[Title]
[Company]
[Optional: phone or LinkedIn — only if relevant]
```

Rules:
- No motivational quotes, graphics, or legal disclaimers unless required by company policy
- Don't repeat your email address — they already have it
- Keep it under 4 lines

---

## Output Template

When writing or evaluating an email, use this structure as your checklist:

```
Subject: [Specific, 6–10 words, accurate expectation]

Hi [First name],

[Why you're reaching out — context + why this person, 2–3 sentences]

[The specifics — timeline, scope, ask, constraints, 2–3 sentences]

[One-sentence CTA — single action, low friction]

[Name]
[Title]
[Company]
```

---

## Evaluation Checklist

When reviewing a draft email, check:

**Subject Line**
- [ ] Specific — does it say what the email is actually about?
- [ ] Under 10 words?
- [ ] No bait-and-switch?

**Body**
- [ ] Starts with substance, not filler?
- [ ] Clear reason why *this person* is receiving it?
- [ ] Specifics included (timeline, scope, ask)?
- [ ] No sentence that could be deleted without losing meaning?

**Closing**
- [ ] Exactly one CTA?
- [ ] Low friction — easy to say yes to?

**Signature**
- [ ] Name, title, company — and nothing unnecessary?

## Common Antipatterns

| Antipattern | Why it fails | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "I hope this finds you well" | Filler — delays substance | Delete it, start with the point |
| Multiple CTAs | Creates decision paralysis | Pick one action |
| Vague subject lines | Low open rate | Be specific about what the email contains |
| Long paragraphs | Kills readability | 3 sentences max per paragraph |
| No salutation | Feels abrupt or automated | Always open with their name |
| Overselling in the closing | Feels desperate | The body sells; the close just directs |
