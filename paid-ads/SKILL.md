# Paid Ads Skill

Four problems must be solved before paid ads produce reliable results:

1. **Where do I advertise?** — Pick the right platform
2. **Who sees it?** — Target the right audience
3. **What does it say?** — Build the ad: Callout + Value + CTA
4. **How do I capture them?** — Get permission to follow up

Solve all four. A great ad on the wrong platform fails. A great ad with bad targeting fails. A great ad with no clear CTA fails.

---

## Problem 1 — Where to Advertise

**Rule:** Pick a platform where your ideal audience already spends time, where you can afford to place ads, and where you understand the format.

Checklist before picking a platform:
- [ ] Can I target people interested in what I sell?
- [ ] Do I know the ad format (image, video, search, audio)?
- [ ] Do I have enough budget to test properly?

**Pro tip:** Don't start from scratch. Place ads where your competitors are already advertising. Replicate before you iterate — if they're spending there, there's already proof the audience converts.

---

## Problem 2 — Targeting

Get the right people to see your ad. Most platforms let you target by some combination of:

- Income
- Gender
- Age
- Location
- Interests / intent signals
- Time of day / day of week

Start with direct demographic and interest targeting. Once you have data and conversions, layer in **lookalike audiences** — the platform finds more people who resemble your existing buyers.

Available targeting options are platform-dependent. What works on Meta won't map 1:1 to Google or LinkedIn. Learn the targeting tools of your chosen platform before launching.

## Problem 3 — Build the Ad

Every ad has three components, in this order:

```
CALLOUT → VALUE → CTA
```

### Component A — Callout

**The callout is the most important part of your ad.** 80% of your ad budget is spent the moment someone reads or hears your headline. If they stop there, nothing else matters. The first 5 seconds must earn the next 25.

Goal: get the right people to stop, and repel everyone else.

#### Verbal callouts — using words to grab attention

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Label** | A word or phrase that puts people into a group they identify with | "Clark County Moms" / "Gym Owners" / "Remote Workers" |
| **Yes-question** | A question where answering yes means "this is for me" | "Do you wake up to pee more than once a night?" |
| **If-then statement** | A condition → payoff structure | "If you run over $1,000/mo in ads, we can save you 20% or more" |
| **Ridiculous result** | Bizarre, rare, or counterintuitive outcome the audience wants | "This woman lost 50 lbs eating pizza and fired her trainer" |

Local + label combos are one of the highest-performing verbal callout structures. A city name + type of person = instant relevance for everyone in that group.

#### Non-verbal callouts — using visuals to grab attention

| Type | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Contrast** | Anything that stands out in the first few frames | Bright shirt vs. dull, movement vs. stillness, loud sound vs. silence |
| **Likeness** | Show people who look, talk, and act like your audience | Broad audience → diverse cast. Niche audience → match their demographic exactly |
| **Quack like a duck** | Dress, talk, and appear as your audience expects | Plumber targeting plumbers → look like a plumber, be in a plumbing environment |
| **Mascot** | Character who represents the brand | Doesn't age, never asks for a raise, never takes a day off |
| **Scene** | Visual situation your audience recognizes as their own problem | Person tossing in bed → sleep-deprived audience; pear shape → body type callout |

#### Infinite Ads system

Record 30+ opening 5-second clips (callouts only) and 10 full ads. Mix and match. You now have 300 ad variations you can test in hours. Apply the best-performing callouts to all your full ads.

### Component B — Value

Once they notice the ad, they need a reason to act. If the benefits look big and the costs look small, they'll exchange their contact info or money. If costs outweigh benefits, they won't.

**Use the What / Who / When framework to generate every angle your offer has.**

#### THE WHAT — Eight dimensions of value

For each dimension, show the upside of buying AND the downside of not buying:

| Dimension | Show This | And Its Opposite |
|---|---|---|
| **Dream outcome** | The maximum result they can achieve | The nightmare of going without |
| **Likelihood** | Why they'll succeed (testimonials, authority, guarantees) | The risk of continued failure |
| **Speed** | How much faster they get results with you | How slow their current path is |
| **Ease** | How little work or skill is required | How much effort they're wasting right now |

In every case: make the benefit as large as possible. Make the cost (risk, time, effort) as small as possible.

#### THE WHO — Status change

People are status-driven. If your offer changes how others treat your customer, show it. Identify:

1. **The person gaining status** — your customer
2. **The people giving it** — spouse, kids, colleagues, boss, rivals, friends

Example: "Risk-free means your spouse won't nag you about the purchase. Your competitors notice your business is growing. Your friend at the golf course says business must be good when you pull up in the new car."

Spell out the status implications — don't assume they'll connect the dots.

#### THE WHEN — Past, present, future

Show consequences across time — from their perspective, and from the perspective of people they care about:

- **Past:** How did this problem start? What old wound does it connect to?
- **Present:** What are they experiencing right now?
- **Future:** Where does this path lead if nothing changes?

Example (weight loss): Getting teased as a kid (past) → struggling to button their favorite jeans (present) → moving up another belt loop (future). Then show it from their kid's perspective. Their spouse's.

Combine What + Who + When to generate as many advertising angles as possible. Each intersection is a distinct ad.

---

### Component C — CTA

Tell them exactly what to do next. One action. Make it obvious.

Examples:
- "Click the button below"
- "Call this number"
- "Reply with YES"
- "Go to [website]"
- "Scan this QR code"

**Rules:**
- One CTA per ad. Don't give them choices.
- Make the mechanic frictionless: obvious button, short URL, simple form.
- Urgency matters — their motivation is highest right now, not tomorrow.

For offer and lead magnet design (what you're sending them toward), read `lead-magnets/SKILL.md`.

## Problem 4 — Get Permission to Contact Them

After they click, capture their contact info. Keep the landing page simple — match the ad's message exactly so there's no cognitive gap.

**Landing page rules:**
- Match the headline and visual of the ad
- One clear offer, one form
- Fewer fields = higher conversion (name + email is usually enough to start)
- All the offer detail goes on the lead magnet page, not here

The action they take — filling out the form — turns them into an engaged lead.

## Evaluation Checklist

When reviewing an ad or ad campaign, check each of these:

**Platform & targeting**
- [ ] Is the platform one where the ICP already spends time?
- [ ] Is targeting narrow enough to reach the right people?
- [ ] Is the budget sufficient to generate meaningful test data?

**Callout**
- [ ] Does the first 5 seconds earn continued attention?
- [ ] Is there a verbal or visual callout that speaks directly to the ICP?
- [ ] Does the callout repel non-buyers as effectively as it attracts buyers?

**Value**
- [ ] Is the dream outcome shown, not just described?
- [ ] Is the nightmare of not acting shown?
- [ ] Is there a status angle (how does this change how others treat the customer)?
- [ ] Does the ad show past, present, or future consequences?

**CTA**
- [ ] Is there exactly one action being asked for?
- [ ] Is the mechanic obvious and frictionless?

**Landing page**
- [ ] Does the landing page match the ad's headline and visual?
- [ ] Is the form as short as possible?

## How the Four Problems Connect

```
Right platform + right targeting → ad reaches the right strangers
↓
Callout → they notice it
↓
Value (What/Who/When) → they want what you offer
↓
CTA → they take an action
↓
Landing page → they become an engaged lead
```

All four must work. Most failed ad campaigns break at callout (wrong first 5 seconds) or targeting (right message, wrong audience). Diagnose from the top down.
