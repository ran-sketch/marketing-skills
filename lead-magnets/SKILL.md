# Lead Magnet Skill (Alex Hormozi — 100M Leads Framework)

## Core Concepts to Always Keep in Mind

**Engaged lead:** A person who shows interest by giving contact info, following, or replying. Raw leads don't matter — engaged leads do.

**The trust funnel:**
```
100 low-trust leads → Lead Magnet (mini offer, free) → 80 engaged leads → Core Offer → 3 buyers
```

**The lead magnet's job:** Solve a narrow problem for free. Doing so reveals the bigger problem — which only your core offer solves. The lead magnet must be valuable enough that you *could* charge for it, even though you don't.

**Rule:** Give away the secrets. Sell the implementation.

---

## The 7-Step Framework

### Step 1 — Pick the Problem and the Person

- Start with a **narrow, meaningful problem** (not a broad one).
- Ask: "Can solving this narrow problem reveal a larger problem my core offer solves?" If yes, you have a winner.
- Example: Helping homeowners sell their house is broad. Helping them get better listing photos, staging, or landscaping is narrow — and solving those makes the main problem (selling the house) more obvious.
- For B2B example: "How to segment your ICP" is a narrow free offer. "How to build and track it inside your CRM" is the paid core offer.

**Prompt the user to answer:**
1. Who is your ideal customer?
2. What is one narrow problem they face *before* they need your core offer?
3. Does solving it make them want your core offer more?

---

### Step 2 — Choose How to Solve It

Pick one of three approaches:

| Approach | What it does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Reveal the problem** | Diagnose something that gets worse the longer they wait | Free audit, assessment, data report |
| **Sample / trial** | Give full but brief access to the core offer | Free trial, speed demo, done-for-you sample |
| **One step of a multi-step process** | Give one valuable step; the rest requires buying | Template, calculator, guide, module |

**Which to use:**
- Use "reveal" when the customer doesn't know they have the problem.
- Use "sample" when the product speaks for itself and quick value is obvious.
- Use "one step" when your core offer involves a complex process (finance, ops, marketing systems).

---

### Step 3 — Choose the Delivery Format

| Format | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Software/tool** | Spreadsheet, calculator, app, widget |
| **Education** | Course, guide, live event, interview, tips/hacks, mistakes to avoid |
| **Service** | Free audit, free ad run, free strategy session |
| **Physical product** | Sample, supplement, printed guide |

Match the format to what the audience actually consumes. Don't default to "webinar" — people want the output, not the format.

---

### Step 4 — Name It

**The name is the most important part of packaging.** People are 5x more likely to read a headline than anything else.

Rules for a good lead magnet name:
- Be specific about the result (not vague)
- Include the audience or situation when possible
- Use plain language — no jargon
- A/B test before committing

**Ways to test names:**
- Run a poll on social media (option 1 vs. option 2)
- Post on all platforms, ask for comments
- Message people directly and ask which they'd click

Always test at least 2 name options before launching.

**Name formula (use as a starting point):**
> [Specific outcome] for [specific person/situation] without [common objection or pain]

Example: "How to Double Booked Demos in 30 Days Without Hiring More SDRs"

---

### Step 5 — Make It Easy to Consume

- Available on **mobile and desktop**
- Offer **multiple formats** when possible: written, audio, video
- Keep it short enough to actually finish
- Don't gate consumption behind unnecessary friction

---

### Step 6 — Make It Good

**Give away the secrets. Sell the implementation.**

- Don't hold back the "what" and "why" — give it freely
- The "how to do it yourself, step by step, at scale" is what you charge for
- Quality builds trust faster than quantity

If the lead magnet is weak, reduce it to one narrower thing and do that one thing extremely well.

---

### Step 7 — Make It Easy for Them to Say They Want More

Every lead magnet needs a **CTA (Call to Action)**.

**A good CTA has two components:**
1. **What to do** — clear, direct, simple language ("Book a call", "Reply to this email", "Click here")
2. **Two reasons to do it right now** — urgency or scarcity

**Urgency vs. Scarcity:**
- **Scarcity** = limited quantity ("I only take 5 new clients per month")
- **Urgency** = limited time ("Offer expires Friday")

Don't say "Don't delay." Say "Call now." Direct > clever.

## When the User Brings a Real Lead Magnet to Evaluate

Run through these checks and give direct, honest feedback:

1. Is the problem narrow enough? (If not, help them narrow it.)
2. Does solving it reveal the core offer's value? (If not, pick a different narrow problem.)
3. Is the delivery format matched to what the audience actually consumes?
4. Is the name specific, testable, and result-oriented?
5. Is there a clear CTA with urgency or scarcity?
6. Could you charge for this? If not, it's not good enough yet.

---

## When the User Asks You to Create a Lead Magnet From Scratch

Ask these questions if not already answered:
1. What is your core offer? (Product/service + who it's for)
2. What does your ideal customer struggle with *before* they need your core offer?
3. Do you have existing content, tools, or processes you could package?

Then produce:
- A narrow problem statement
- Recommended delivery format with rationale
- 2–3 name options (to be A/B tested)
- A draft CTA

---

## Common Mistakes to Flag

- **Too broad:** "The Ultimate Guide to Marketing" — no one clicks this
- **No trust gap:** Giving the full solution for free removes the reason to buy
- **Weak CTA:** "Learn more" is not a CTA. It tells them nothing.
- **Format mismatch:** A 90-minute webinar when the audience wants a 1-page checklist
- **No testing:** Naming something without testing it is leaving money on the table
