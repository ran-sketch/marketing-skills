# Content Framework Skill

Apply the Hook → Retain → Reward framework to create high-performing content across any platform or format.

---

## Core Principle

The smallest unit of content that hooks, retains, and rewards attention is a **content unit** — it can be as small as a meme, a sentence, or a jingle. Every content unit must do all three jobs simultaneously. This is how short tweets, meme images, or jingles go viral.

Free content serves three business purposes:
- Grows a warm audience more likely to buy
- Makes all other advertising more effective
- Builds credibility when prospects look you up

---

## The Three-Part Framework

### 1. HOOK — Give them a reason to pay attention

**Objective:** Pull the audience away from whatever they're doing and toward your content.

#### Pick a topic from one of five categories:

| Category | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Far Past** | Important life lessons. Give the story without the skill. | "I complained to a friend I had no time — they grabbed my phone and showed me I spent 3hrs/day on social media." |
| **Recent Past** | Things you did or conversations you had recently. | Any call, meeting, or interaction with a takeaway the audience can use. |
| **Present** | Ideas captured in the moment. Always have a way to record ideas in arm's reach. | Text/email ideas to yourself mid-meeting. Build a bucket of fresh ideas. |
| **Trending** | What's getting attention right now. Apply your experiences or expertise to it. | Only use if you have a relevant angle or commentary. |
| **Manufactured** | Pick an interesting topic → learn about it → make/do it → show the world. | Turn ideas into reality, then document the process. |

#### Write a headline using at least 2 of these 7 components:

| Component | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Recency** | As recent as possible — something that happened an hour ago beats a year ago |
| **Relevance** | Personally meaningful to your specific audience (nurses care about nurse content) |
| **Celebrity** | Prominent people, authorities, or well-known entities (Jeff Bezos's breakfast matters; yours doesn't) |
| **Proximity** | Close to home geographically — neighbor's house fire vs. one across the country |
| **Conflict** | Opposing ideas or people — pineapple on pizza, hero vs. villain, freedom vs. security |
| **Unusual** | Odd, unique, bizarre — outside the norm gets more attention |
| **Ongoing** | Dynamic, evolving stories with plot twists — people want updates because anything could happen |

#### Match the format to the platform:

Format your content to match what performs best on that platform — what the audience expects to be rewarded by. The same person posts differently on LinkedIn (professional, serious), Facebook (celebratory, social), Instagram (visual, lifestyle), etc.

**Action:** Format for the platform first, then tweak to hook your ideal audience. Use top-performing content in your niche as the benchmark.

---

### 2. RETAIN — Keep them reading/watching

**Objective:** Embed unresolved questions in the audience's mind so they stay until the end.

The best driver of retention is **curiosity**. Done right, people will wait years to know what happens next. Unresolved questions can be explicit (directly asked) or implicit (implied).

Use one or more of these retention structures:

**Lists**
- State the number upfront: "Top 10 mistakes…" or "5 big money makers…"
- The number sets expectations and gives people a reason to stay for all items

**Steps**
- Actions that occur in order and accomplish a goal when completed
- If early steps are clear and valuable, the reader wants to know all of them
- Example: "3 steps to creating a great hook"

**Stories**
- Describe events, real or imaginary — stories worth telling have a lesson or takeaway
- Can be what happened, what might have happened, or what will never happen
- All three drive curiosity because people want to know what happens next

**Action:** Use lists, steps, or stories (or combine them) to keep the audience curious. Embed questions in their mind that make them want to know what comes next.

---

### 3. REWARD — Deliver on the promise

**Objective:** Provide more value than anyone else. Fully resolve what the hook promised.

Completely answer the unresolved questions you embedded. If your hook promised a lesson, deliver it. If it promised a story resolution, land it. If it promised a list of 5 things, give all 5.

**The rule:** Make good on every promise. The reward must match the hook — don't bait and switch.

## Monetization — The Give:Ask Ratio

**Motto: Give, give, give, give, give, give until they ask.**

Content builds an account of goodwill. Offers withdraw from it. You must deposit before you can withdraw.

| Platform stage | Minimum give:ask ratio |
|---|---|
| Mature (TV-equivalent) | 3.5:1 — 47 min give, 13 min ask per hour |
| Growing (you, right now) | 10:1 — at least 10 gives before first ask |

**The rule:** The moment you start asking for money is the moment you decide to slow down your growth. The more patient you are, the more you'll get out of it when you finally make your ask.

Think of your asks as **commercials**. You interrupt your own programming with a message about the thing you sell. You pay for it with potential loss of trust, slowed growth, and the audience capital you built.

### Two Promotion Strategies

**Integrated Offers**
Promote in every piece of content as long as the give:ask ratio stays high. A 1-hour podcast with 3 × 30-second ads = 58.5 minutes giving / 1.5 minutes asking. Continuous growth and engaged leads. Win-win.

**Intermittent Offers**
Make 10 pure-give posts, then make 1 ask post. The ask is either:
- Your core offer directly
- A lead magnet CTA ("I have 11 more tips on my website — click the link")

Use whichever strategy fits your volume. Both work. The ratio is what matters.

## The 7 Hormozi Content Laws

**1. "How I" not "How to"**
Switch from prescribing to sharing. "How I built my 7-figure agency" not "How to build a 7-figure agency." "My favorite ways to do X" not "The best way to do X." When you share your experience, you deliver value. When you prescribe, you trigger resistance. The shift is subtle but the reception is very different.

**2. Repeat yourself**
Your audience needs to be reminded more than taught. You will get bored of your own content before your entire audience has even seen it once. Keep repeating your best ideas. New people are always arriving. Existing people forget. Say it again.

**3. Puddles → Ponds → Lakes → Oceans**
Narrow your content focus. Don't try to cover everything. The more specific and focused your content angle, the more it compounds with the right audience. Breadth feels like reach; specificity is what actually builds authority.

**4. Content creates sales tools**
Build a master list of your greatest hits. Label each piece with: the problem it solves + the benefit it delivers. Then your sales team (or you) can send the exact right piece before or after a sales call to help prospects decide. Content is not just audience-building — it's a sales asset library.

**5. Free content retains paying customers**
People who buy your stuff are more likely to consume your free content — not less. They factor it into how they calculate their ROI from your paid product. If your free content is weak, it hurts retention. If it's strong, it reinforces the decision to stay.

**6. Higher standards, not shorter attention spans**
The audience hasn't lost the ability to pay attention. They've raised their standards. They will watch long content. They will read long posts. They just won't sit through boring content.

**7. There's no such thing as too long — only too boring**
Length is never the real problem. Quality is. If you're losing people, it's not because the content is long. It's because it stopped being worth their time.

## How to Use This Skill

### Writing new content
1. Identify the platform and audience
2. Choose a topic category (Far Past / Recent Past / Present / Trending / Manufactured)
3. Write a headline using at least 2 of the 7 components
4. Choose a retention structure (list, steps, or story)
5. Deliver a reward that fully resolves the hook

### Improving existing content
Score the content on each component:
- **Hook:** Does it have a topic category? Does the headline use 2+ components? Is it formatted for the platform?
- **Retain:** Does it embed an unresolved question? Does it use a list, steps, or story?
- **Reward:** Does it fully deliver on what the hook promised?

Output: scores (Strong / Needs Work / Missing) + specific suggestions + revised version.

### Brainstorming content ideas
Generate ideas across all five topic categories for the user's niche, then apply headline components to make each one attention-grabbing.

### Consulting mode (what would Alex say?)
When the user asks for an opinion on content strategy, promotion timing, or content quality — respond as Alex Hormozi would: direct, no fluff, grounded in the frameworks above.

Apply in this order:
1. **Is the give:ask ratio right?** Count gives vs. asks. If they're promoting before earning the ratio, say so directly.
2. **Which of the 7 laws applies?** Identify the relevant law(s) and name them.
3. **What's the one move?** Give one concrete action — not a list of options.

Voice guidance: Hormozi is blunt, numbers-first, doesn't soften bad news, doesn't use corporate language. He says "this won't work because X" not "you might consider Y." Match that energy.
